\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Overview}

Adaptive TACC is a three-stage policy framework built on top of a mobility-derived graph and a node-specific demand matrix. The first stage constructs candidate placements, including standard baselines and a topology-aware greedy placement. The second stage refines the greedy placement using DQN-based local search when refinement is justified. The third stage selects among strong candidate policies using held-out perturbation samples and a computation-cost penalty. The method is designed around the observation that different perturbation regimes favor different placement behavior and different optimization budgets.

\subsection{Graph Construction}

The mobility trace is represented as sequences of AP associations. Consecutive AP changes by the same client create handoff events. Handoff counts are aggregated over all clients to form a weighted undirected graph. Each edge stores a raw handoff count, a normalized redundancy weight, and an inverse-count latency weight. Frequent handoffs therefore imply strong relatedness and low cooperative retrieval cost. The largest active component is used for evaluation so that cooperative retrieval is defined over a connected graph.

This graph construction makes the cooperation topology data-dependent. Two APs are adjacent because clients move between them, not because a fixed radius or manually chosen neighborhood says they should cooperate. The resulting topology captures the behavioral structure that is relevant for caching: where demand may migrate and where cooperative retrieval is likely to be useful.

\subsection{Demand Construction}

Content demand is generated using a global Zipf distribution and AP-level locality. Let \(q_c\propto c^{-\alpha}\) denote global catalog popularity. For each AP community, a subset of objects receives a locality boost, after which probabilities are normalized to produce \(P_{i,c}\). This creates demand distributions that share a global popularity shape but differ across APs. The locality multiplier controls how strongly node demand deviates from the global distribution.

This demand model is not intended to replace real content logs. It is a controlled way to evaluate caching behavior when the mobility trace contains association events but not object requests. The model also supports a meaningful comparison among local popularity, global popularity, diversified placement, and topology-aware placement because the policies face both common popular objects and location-specific preferences.

\subsection{Topology Perturbation}

For perturbation rate \(p\), each node is removed with probability \(p\), and each edge is removed with probability \(p/2\). The largest connected component is retained. This sampler creates progressively less stable cooperative graphs while preserving enough structure for evaluation. Candidate policies are evaluated over repeated samples so that objective mean and variance can be measured.

The perturbation model is intentionally simple, but it isolates an important effect. A placement optimized for the nominal graph may rely on central nodes or short paths that disappear under perturbation. A more diverse placement may survive moderate perturbation better because more objects remain reachable. The selector uses this difference to choose among candidate policies and to decide whether DQN refinement is worth its added cost.

The perturbation rate should be interpreted as an operating-regime descriptor rather than as a permanent physical failure probability. A larger \(p\) means that the controller sees fewer active APs and fewer reliable cooperative links in a validation window. This changes access paths, origin misses, redundancy relationships, and the value of replicas. The experiments stop at \(p=0.15\) because the research question is how to adapt while cooperation still exists. Beyond that range, many sampled graphs can become dominated by disconnected or near-local behavior, and the system should trigger topology re-estimation or a fallback placement mode rather than assuming that the same cooperative objective remains equally informative.

\subsection{Baseline Policies}

The baseline set is designed to avoid weak comparisons. Random placement measures the value of unstructured diversity. Local popularity measures independent AP optimization. Global popularity measures the effect of storing the same popular objects everywhere. Diversified popularity shifts popular-object slices across APs to increase cooperative catalog coverage. Topology-aware greedy measures direct optimization of the proposed objective. Greedy plus DQN measures learning-based refinement around a structured warm start. Adaptive TACC measures regime-aware and cost-aware selection among strong candidates.

\subsection{Topology-Aware Greedy Placement}

The greedy algorithm starts with an empty placement and repeatedly adds the feasible node-content pair with the largest expected objective reduction. Candidate gains are estimated over sampled perturbed graphs:
\[
\Delta(i,c|x)=
\mathbb{E}_{\tilde{G}\sim\mathcal{P}_p(G)}
\left[J(x,\tilde{G})-J(x^{(i,c)},\tilde{G})\right],
\]
where \(x^{(i,c)}\) is the placement obtained by adding content \(c\) to node \(i\). Candidate contents are restricted to high-demand objects at each node to keep marginal-gain evaluation tractable.

This stage is one of the main contributions because the marginal gain is not computed from popularity alone. A candidate object is valuable when it reduces local or cooperative access cost, avoids unnecessary origin retrieval, and does not create excessive duplicate storage among strongly related APs. The same object may therefore be attractive at a weakly connected AP but unattractive at a strongly related neighbor that can already retrieve it cheaply. Sampling perturbed graphs inside the gain estimate also discourages placements that depend too heavily on a single central holder.

The candidate restriction is a computational device, not a change in the optimization objective. For each AP, the algorithm considers objects with high local demand and high global demand, then scores those feasible pairs using the full objective. This preserves the important object choices while avoiding a full scan of the \(N|\mathcal{C}|\) action space at every greedy step. Since each node has finite cache capacity, the loop stops after no feasible pair provides positive expected reduction or after all cache slots are filled.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Topology-Aware Greedy Placement}
\label{alg:greedy_final}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Graph \(G\), demand \(P\), capacity \(K\), perturbation sampler \(\mathcal{P}_p\)
\State Initialize placement \(x\gets 0\)
\While{some node has free cache capacity}
    \State bestGain \(\gets 0\), bestPair \(\gets \varnothing\)
    \For{each feasible node-content pair \((i,c)\)}
        \State Estimate \(\Delta(i,c|x)\) over sampled perturbations
        \If{\(\Delta(i,c|x)>\) bestGain}
            \State bestGain \(\gets \Delta(i,c|x)\), bestPair \(\gets (i,c)\)
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \If{bestPair is empty}
        \State \textbf{break}
    \EndIf
    \State Add bestPair to placement \(x\)
\EndWhile
\State \Return \(x\)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{DQN Refinement}

The DQN refiner treats cache modification as a local sequential decision problem. The state concatenates the binary placement matrix, the demand matrix, and graph features. Graph features include normalized degree, weighted degree, and closeness centrality. The action space is a node-content toggle. If adding an object to a full cache would violate capacity, the lowest-demand cached object at that node is evicted. The reward is objective improvement:
\[
r_t=J(x_t,\tilde{G}_t)-J(x_{t+1},\tilde{G}_t).
\]

The DQN uses two hidden layers with ReLU activations, replay-buffer sampling, a target network, discount factor 0.92, learning rate 0.001, and epsilon-greedy exploration. During inference, an action is accepted only if it improves the measured objective. This accepted-action rule is critical because it prevents the learned component from degrading the greedy warm start. In effect, the neural network proposes local modifications, and the objective acts as a safety filter.

The learning component is therefore deliberately bounded. It is not trained to construct a full placement from scratch, which would make the action space too large and the result difficult to interpret. Instead, the DQN learns where the greedy solution has local weaknesses under sampled perturbations. A useful action may replace a redundant object, introduce a replica at a more resilient node, or reduce expected cooperative path length. If the proposed move fails the objective check, the placement remains unchanged. This design makes learning part of the adaptive method while preserving the reproducibility and stability of the structured optimizer.

\subsection{Adaptive TACC Selector}

Adaptive TACC selects among diversified popularity, topology-aware greedy, and DQN-refined greedy. The selector is designed to reduce amortized complexity. It first evaluates the cheaper diversified and greedy candidates. DQN refinement is considered only when the cheap candidates are close in score or when the best cheap candidate has high validation variability. This prevents the system from paying DQN refinement cost when a cheaper placement is already reliable.

The selector answers a different question from the placement algorithms. Diversified popularity asks whether broad catalog coverage is enough. Topology-aware greedy asks whether directly optimizing the stochastic objective is enough. DQN refinement asks whether local learned substitutions improve a strong warm start. Adaptive TACC asks which of those answers is justified in the current perturbation regime after accounting for validation quality, variability, replication pressure, and optimization overhead.

For each candidate policy, the selector computes
\[
S(\pi)=\bar{J}_{\text{val}}(\pi)+\alpha\sigma_{\text{val}}(\pi)+\rho p^2 R(\pi)+\eta\Omega(\pi)-\kappa U(\pi),
\]
where \(\bar{J}_{\text{val}}\) is validation objective, \(\sigma_{\text{val}}\) is validation standard deviation, \(R(\pi)\) is average replication cost, \(\Omega(\pi)\) is a normalized optimization-cost estimate, and \(U(\pi)\) is an instability credit applied to DQN when the best cheap candidate is highly variable. The \(p^2\) term makes replication pressure grow smoothly with perturbation intensity rather than using a fixed regime switch. The selected policy is
\[
\pi^*=\arg\min_{\pi}S(\pi).
\]

This rule keeps selection dynamic rather than fixed. When the graph is stable, a small DQN improvement can be rejected because the compute and relocation costs outweigh the marginal objective reduction. Under moderate perturbation, diversified placement can be selected because broader content coverage survives missing nodes and edges. Under higher churn, DQN can be selected if validation samples show that cheap candidates are unstable and learned local substitutions reduce objective enough to justify their cost. The policy choice is therefore learned from validation evidence in each regime, not hard-coded by perturbation rate.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Adaptive TACC Policy Selection}
\label{alg:adaptive_selector}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Cheap policies \(\Pi_0\), optional DQN policy \(\pi_D\), perturbation rate \(p\), validation sampler \(\mathcal{V}_p\)
\For{each policy \(\pi \in \Pi_0\)}
    \State Evaluate \(\pi\) on held-out perturbation samples from \(\mathcal{V}_p\)
    \State Compute cost-aware score \(S(\pi)\)
\EndFor
\If{cheap scores are close or best cheap score is unstable}
    \State Train/evaluate DQN-refined policy \(\pi_D\)
    \State Compute \(S(\pi_D)\) including optimization cost and instability credit
\EndIf
\State \Return policy with minimum computed score \(S(\pi)\)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Computational Complexity}

The greedy stage is the most expensive deterministic component. If there are \(N\) nodes, \(M\) content objects, cache capacity \(K\), and \(S\) perturbation samples per marginal evaluation, naive greedy search can require \(O(NKMNS)\) objective evaluations. Candidate filtering reduces the effective value of \(M\) by considering only high-demand objects at each node. The DQN action space has size \(NM\), and training cost scales with the number of episodes, steps per episode, and minibatch size. Since DQN starts from the greedy placement, it does not need to learn an entire placement from an empty cache state.

The selector adds validation overhead for a small set of candidate policies, but it can reduce amortized cost by avoiding DQN refinement in regimes where cheaper policies are clearly sufficient. In the final run, DQN is considered for two of the four perturbation rates and selected for one of them. This behavior is more efficient than invoking the most expensive refinement path in every regime.
