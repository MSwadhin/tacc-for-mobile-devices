Edge caching reduces delay and backhaul load by placing content near users, but IoT edge networks are difficult caching environments because user mobility changes both demand and the effective cooperation topology among edge nodes. This paper studies topology-aware cooperative caching in a graph-based IoT edge system where access points act as edge caches and mobility-induced handoffs define weighted inter-node relationships. The work formulates cache placement as a stochastic optimization problem that jointly considers access cost, replication overhead, topology-aware redundancy, and relocation cost under random topology perturbations. To solve the problem, the paper proposes Adaptive TACC, a reproducible pipeline that combines topology-aware greedy placement, DQN-based local refinement, and a validation-based selector that chooses the most suitable placement strategy for the current perturbation regime. Because the Dartmouth campus WLAN trace is publicly described but not directly downloadable through legacy unauthenticated URLs during this implementation, the repository records the official dataset access path and provides a deterministic trace-compatible fallback for reproducibility. Experiments over a 20-node mobility graph, 48-object catalog, and repeated perturbation samples show that no single static strategy is best across all regimes: DQN-refined greedy performs best under nominal and high-churn conditions, while diversity-aware placement is stronger under moderate perturbation. Adaptive TACC selects between these behaviors and achieves the best or tied-best objective at all tested perturbation rates, reducing the nominal objective from 2.633 for topology-aware greedy to 2.414 and maintaining a 0.994 hit ratio.
