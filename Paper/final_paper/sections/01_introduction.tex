\section{Introduction}

Edge caching is a core mechanism for reducing user-perceived latency, lowering backhaul traffic, and improving service availability in IoT and edge-computing systems. By storing frequently requested content at gateways, access points, roadside units, or small cells, edge networks can serve requests closer to where they originate. This is especially important for latency-sensitive applications such as augmented reality, autonomous mobility, real-time sensing, and local analytics \cite{Satyanarayanan2017Edge,Chiang2016Fog,Bastug2014}.

Classical caching strategies often assume that each cache can be optimized independently or that cooperation relationships among caches are fixed. These assumptions are weak for IoT edge systems. Users move between access points, workloads vary across location and time, and effective cooperation changes as devices associate with different parts of the network. Even when the physical infrastructure is stationary, the logical interaction graph changes because demand migrates with users. A cache placement that is efficient for one mobility regime may become inefficient when AP availability, handoff frequency, or local demand changes.

We treat cooperative edge caching as a topology-aware optimization problem. Edge nodes are modeled as vertices in a weighted graph, where edge weights encode mobility-induced interaction strength. If users frequently move between two APs, those APs are likely to observe correlated demand and can benefit from coordinated caching. The objective is not simply to maximize local cache hits. Instead, it balances four costs: expected access latency, replication overhead, topology-aware redundancy among strongly related nodes, and relocation cost when the cache is changed after perturbation.

We also address a practical problem in public WLAN traces: mobility traces often do not contain application-level content request logs. To support controlled and reproducible evaluation, demand is synthesized using a Zipf distribution with spatial locality. Zipf-like content popularity is a standard model in caching research \cite{breslau1999zipf,Podlipnig2003}, and the spatial-locality component makes AP demand distributions related but not identical.

The proposed system, Adaptive TACC, combines three ideas. First, a topology-aware greedy algorithm builds a strong initial placement by choosing node-content pairs with high expected marginal benefit under perturbations. Second, a compact DQN refiner searches around the greedy placement and accepts only changes that improve the measured objective. Third, an adaptive selector evaluates candidate placements on held-out perturbation samples and chooses the policy most appropriate for the current churn regime. This final selector is important because the experiments show that no single static policy dominates under all perturbation rates.

The design intentionally avoids two common extremes. A purely heuristic policy is easy to run but may not adapt when the topology changes. A purely learning-based policy can, in principle, adapt, but training directly over all binary cache placements is expensive and unstable. Adaptive TACC uses the greedy stage to obtain a good structured placement, the DQN stage to search a local improvement neighborhood, and the selector stage to choose between coverage-oriented and stability-oriented placements. This layered design gives the system both interpretability and adaptability.

The study separates measured claims from modeling assumptions. The topology pipeline is compatible with Dartmouth-style AP movement traces; the evaluation uses a deterministic trace-compatible mobility generator because the raw Dartmouth movement archive was not directly downloadable through legacy unauthenticated URLs at the time of this study. Content demand is synthetic and explicitly parameterized. These choices are limitations, but they also make the experiment reproducible and transparent.

The contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a topology-aware cooperative caching formulation that integrates mobility-derived graph structure, demand uncertainty, redundancy, and relocation cost;
    \item a complete reproducible pipeline for trace loading, graph construction, demand generation, greedy placement, DQN refinement, adaptive policy selection, and evaluation;
    \item a research-level gap analysis that identifies common methodology, implementation, dataset, and evaluation gaps in topology-aware edge-caching studies and closes them with concrete design choices;
    \item an experimental study showing that Adaptive TACC achieves the best or tied-best objective across all tested perturbation regimes.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{High-Level Clarification}

The goal of this work is to determine how edge caches should coordinate when the cooperation graph is induced by mobility and changes over time. The algorithm adapts in two ways. First, the DQN refiner modifies a greedy placement by accepting cache substitutions that reduce the objective. Second, the Adaptive TACC selector chooses among DQN-refined greedy, topology-aware greedy, and diversified placement according to held-out perturbation samples. When the graph is perturbed, active nodes and cooperative edges change; as a result, shortest-path retrieval costs, available cache holders, redundancy relationships, hit ratios, and origin misses can all change even if the stored content matrix is fixed. The method is therefore not adapting to an abstract random variable; it adapts to concrete changes in which nodes remain available, which cooperative paths remain usable, and which cached objects can still be reached cheaply.
